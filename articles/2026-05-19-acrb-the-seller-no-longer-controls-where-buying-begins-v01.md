# The Seller No Longer Controls Where Buying Begins

**Deck:** Stripe’s latest push into agent commerce matters less because software can pay, and more because it assumes buying will increasingly start outside the merchant’s own walls.

## The storefront is no longer the starting point

For years, digital retail strategy rested on a simple premise: the merchant’s job was to pull the customer into a branded environment, then optimize the path from interest to checkout. Search, social, marketplaces, and apps complicated that model, but did not quite kill it. The branded site still mattered as the place where intent became visible and conversion could be managed.[1][2]

That arrangement is weakening. Not because chatbots have suddenly become department stores, and not because autonomous shopping agents have already conquered consumer commerce. The stronger, better-supported claim is narrower and more consequential: discovery, evaluation, and even transaction initiation are increasingly happening outside seller surfaces, and the infrastructure of payments is being rebuilt to support that shift.[2][3][4]

Stripe’s 2026 Sessions conference is best read through that lens. The obvious interpretation is that agents can now spend money, which is true enough. But software has long been able to trigger payments under rules and permissions. The more important signal is structural. Stripe, like Visa, Mastercard, PayPal, Microsoft, Google, OpenAI, and others, is investing in a world where payment authority, identity, and fraud controls are abstracted from the merchant’s checkout page and attached instead to wallets, tokens, delegated permissions, and software workflows.[3][5][6][7][8][9][10]

That does not mean the store disappears. It means the store is less likely to be where buying begins.

This distinction matters. Retail executives do not need to believe that consumer agents will soon roam the internet buying sweaters and sofas unprompted. The evidence does not support that. They do need to reckon with a more immediate change: the first commercial decision may increasingly occur in a buyer’s own context, whether that is an AI assistant, a procurement system, a spreadsheet, a messaging thread, or a workflow tool.[4][8][9][11][12] By the time the retailer sees the customer, much of the ranking, filtering, and comparison may already have happened elsewhere.

The old question was how to drive traffic to the store. The emerging one is whether the business is legible and usable when the customer never really enters it at all.

## AI extends a trend that was already underway

It is tempting to treat agentic commerce as a clean break. It is not. Commerce has been moving away from seller entry points for years. Marketplaces aggregated demand. Search engines shaped discovery. Social platforms turned attention into storefronts. Retail media inserted paid influence into the path to purchase. The customer journey became less linear and less owned by any single merchant.[1][2][13]

Generative AI extends that trajectory. It creates new interfaces in which product search, comparison, and task execution can happen in the buyer’s environment rather than the seller’s. OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, and Anthropic all describe their systems as assistants that can retrieve information, compare options, and increasingly take actions through tools and integrations.[8][9][11][14] In enterprise settings, that pattern is already clearer. Procurement and workflow software can surface approved vendors, draft purchase requests, and automate routine buying steps inside governed systems rather than on supplier websites.[12][15]

Consumer retail is murkier. The evidence is strongest for search and discovery disintermediation, weaker for fully autonomous purchase completion at scale.[16][17] Consumers appear willing to use AI for narrowing options and gathering information. They are less obviously ready to hand over final buying authority, especially in categories where fit, taste, trust, and returns matter.[16][17][18]

That is why the most breathless version of the thesis should be resisted. Seller control over where buying begins is weakening, but some of that influence may shift to a new class of intermediaries that govern ranking, identity, and payment rails.[2][19][20] AI assistants could empower users while also becoming gatekeepers in their own right. Retailers have seen a version of this before.

Still, the practical implication is hard to avoid. If consideration begins elsewhere, then the merchant’s site is no longer the sole arena in which demand is formed. A retailer can retain authority over fulfillment, service, merchandising, and post-purchase experience while losing the opening move.

## The real shift is not checkout inside chat

This helps explain why so many conversational commerce experiments have felt underwhelming. Retailers have spent years trying to make transactions happen inside chat. The evidence so far suggests that this was probably the wrong target, or at least an immature one.[16][17][21][22]

Walmart’s public experimentation with generative AI shopping assistants is illustrative rather than definitive. The promise is obvious enough: a customer can describe a need in natural language, get help narrowing options, and build a basket more quickly. The limitation is equally obvious. Chat is often a poor interface for dense assortments, visual comparison, nuanced attributes, substitutions, promotions, and the messy realities of fulfillment and returns.[21][22][18] In categories such as fashion and luxury, where visual presentation, fit, and brand context matter, those weaknesses are especially acute.[18][23]

So the important question is not whether the whole transaction lives inside the chat window. It is whether the decision context has moved. A buyer may use an assistant to compare products, shortlist options, check policies, and decide what to buy, then complete the purchase through more conventional rails. In that world, the retailer still processes the order, but no longer governs the environment in which preference was formed.

That is a subtler and more plausible shift. It also fits the evidence better than the claim that conversational interfaces will replace storefronts wholesale.[16][17][21]

For executives, this changes the strategic target. The task is not merely to bolt a chatbot onto the website. It is to ensure that products, prices, availability, delivery promises, return policies, and trust signals can be interpreted accurately by external systems.[24][25] A merchant that is beautifully merchandised for humans but incoherent to machines may simply be absent from the new consideration set.

## Payment authority is becoming portable

Payments are where this becomes operational. Stripe’s significance lies less in inventing machine spending than in helping normalize a model in which payment authority travels with the user, the wallet, or the task rather than remaining trapped inside a merchant-owned checkout flow.[3][4] Visa and Mastercard have spent years expanding tokenization, network credentials, delegated authentication, and secure remote commerce. PayPal has pushed passkeys, wallet infrastructure, and merchant tools that reduce reliance on manual credential entry.[5][6][7][26][27]

These systems were not built solely for AI agents. They emerged from omnichannel commerce, subscriptions, marketplaces, embedded finance, and the long campaign to reduce friction in digital payments.[4][26][28] Agentic commerce is riding on top of that broader transition. But it does sharpen the stakes. If software is going to act on a user’s behalf, then the critical design question is no longer just whether a payment can be processed. It is who granted authority, under what limits, with what revocation rights, and with what liability if something goes wrong.[5][6][26][28]

This is why the phrase “agents can spend money” is both accurate and misleading. The hard part is not moving funds. The hard part is governing delegated action.

Stablecoins enter the discussion here, though with more caution than enthusiasts usually allow. Stripe and others have expanded stablecoin-related capabilities, and policy institutions such as the Bank for International Settlements, the IMF, and the Financial Stability Board have all examined tokenized money and stablecoin systems.[29][30][31] That makes stablecoins relevant to programmable commerce infrastructure, especially for cross-border settlement, treasury movement, and platform payouts.[29][30] It does not yet make them central to mainstream consumer retail checkout. In the near term, cards and card-linked credentials remain the more established consumer instrument in many markets.[5][6][7][26]

The practical point for retailers is simpler than the debate suggests. Payments strategy is becoming a channel architecture question. Which wallets, credentials, authentication methods, and delegated permissions make the merchant easiest to transact with in external ecosystems? That is no longer a narrow checkout optimization issue. It is part of distribution.

## Fraud will decide the pace of adoption

If there is a binding constraint on agentic commerce, it is not interface design. It is trust.

As commerce becomes more tokenized, API-mediated, and remote, the attack surface shifts. Industry and security research consistently point to rising risks from credential theft, account takeover, synthetic identity, bot abuse, and API exploitation.[32][33][34][35][36] Add AI agents to that mix and the problem becomes sharper. An agent may be legitimately authorized but act outside intended bounds. A token may be stolen or replayed. A workflow may be manipulated. A system may struggle to distinguish between the user, the agent, and a malicious intermediary.[33][34][35][37]

These are not exotic edge cases. They are familiar security problems in a new commercial wrapper.

That is why fraud, identity, and authorization are moving from back-office functions toward the center of strategy. Traditional checkout controls assume a human is present at the moment of purchase. Delegated commerce weakens that assumption. Retailers will need more granular permissions, transaction limits, revocation mechanisms, anomaly detection, and audit trails for machine-initiated actions.[33][35][37]

The unresolved question is who will own the trust layer. Payments networks have one claim. Identity providers and operating systems have another. AI platforms will try to insert themselves. Merchants will not want to surrender too much authority. The eventual market structure is unclear, but the direction of travel is not. The commercial risk is moving from “can the payment clear?” to “who or what was actually authorized to act?”

That shift should make executives wary of superficial pilots. It is easy to launch an AI shopping assistant. It is harder to build the control stack that makes delegated commerce safe enough to scale.

## The winners may be machine-legible retailers

The most immediate operating implication is not glamorous. It is data discipline.

Even without widespread autonomous purchasing, retailers increasingly need machine-readable product catalogs, inventory, pricing, fulfillment options, and policy terms. Search engines, marketplaces, shopping feeds, and product data standards have already pushed the industry in that direction.[24][25][38] AI agents intensify the requirement because poor data quality does not merely reduce conversion on a website. It can exclude a merchant from consideration before a human sees the offer.

Apparel is a good example. Size, fit, fabric, color, care, and availability are often represented inconsistently across systems. Research has long linked poor product data and fit uncertainty to conversion friction and returns.[39][40] In an agent-mediated environment, that becomes a discoverability problem as well as a merchandising one. If a machine cannot reliably compare the offer, it may not surface it.

This points to a more sober conclusion than the original hype around agent commerce. The near-term winners are unlikely to be the retailers with the flashiest AI front ends. They are more likely to be those with the cleanest product data, the clearest policies, the most interoperable payment credentials, and the strongest controls over delegated action.

There is, however, a serious complication. If buying begins outside the merchant’s walls, the merchant may become more dependent on whoever governs the new starting point. AI assistants could become the next search engines: useful, unavoidable, and expensive to negotiate with.[19][20][41][42] Lower-funnel influence may not disappear. It may simply migrate to a new intermediary layer with its own ranking logic and monetization schemes.

That is why the strongest version of the thesis should remain restrained. The evidence supports a world in which sellers lose influence over where consideration begins. It does not yet support a world in which buyers cleanly seize power, or in which autonomous agents become the dominant interface for consumer retail. Those outcomes are possible. They are not proved.

For now, the strategic mistake would be to dismiss the shift because the most theatrical version of it has not arrived. Retailers do not need to prepare for a future in which every customer sends a bot to buy a jacket. They need to prepare for one in which more commercial judgment is made before the customer reaches the jacket page at all.

That is a quieter change than the slogans suggest. It may also be the more durable one.
