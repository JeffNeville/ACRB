Kicker: The store is getting smarter, though less magically than many imagine.

# The Illusion of Visibility

**Deck:** Stores are collecting more data than ever. E-commerce has conditioned retailers to expect real-time insight into each customer; even with AI, in-store signals remain partial and fall short of reliable action.



> “To them, the truth would be literally nothing but the shadows of the images.”
> — Plato, *The Republic*



Retail has spent years building a detailed picture of the online customer. The website knows what was searched, what was saved, what was abandoned, what was bought, and often what might tempt a return. The store offers a flatter picture, dense with signals yet still hard to read. Managers can see traffic. Associates can read some cues. Systems can count transactions. Much of what matters in between still disappears into the aisles.

That gap has become more expensive. Retail labor remains under pressure across major markets. In the United States, retail trade’s annual average quits rate was 2.6 percent in 2025. In the United Kingdom, 16 percent of trading businesses with at least ten employees reported worker shortages in early January 2025. OECD analysis of Europe’s retail sector describes a familiar mix of labor shortages, skills gaps, and high turnover. The store model built for abundant labor and broad human coverage now sits on shakier ground.[1][2][3]

The economics of this are not subtle. Academic research has shown that stores are often understaffed at the wrong hours and that better alignment between traffic and labor can materially improve sales and profit. INFORMS research found that the sales value of traffic rises when labor rises with it. A randomized experiment at Gap found that better scheduling practices increased store productivity by 5.1 percent, with sales up and labor costs down.[4][5]

That is the useful starting point for this chapter. The problem is not that stores lack data. It is that they still lack usable visibility at the moment decisions matter. What is changing now is that retailers can instrument more of the physical environment and connect more of it to action. The stronger claim is narrower than much of the current AI talk.

## The stronger claim is narrower

The original thesis behind the “hidden high-intent customer” is easy to understand. A shopper does her research online, narrows her choices, enters a large or fragmented store, disappears into the layout, and receives no timely help. Labor is scarce. The wrong associate is occupied elsewhere. A high-value sale is missed. The appeal of fixing this with AI is plain enough.

Public evidence suggests the opportunity is real, but the market is earlier than the bolder versions of the thesis imply. What is better supported is that stores can now track traffic, queue build-up, product movement, and inventory state with more precision than before. Some retailers can also identify known customers when those customers choose to be recognized. Workflow tools can push alerts and prompts to associates. The more ambitious leap, from better store observability to reliable real-time detection of an anonymous high-intent shopper and then to automated labor orchestration, remains much less proven.[6][7][8][9][10][11][12]

The distinction matters because retail has a habit of letting one promising tool stand in for a full operating model. Generative AI is the current favorite candidate. McKinsey’s 2024 survey of large retailers found heavy experimentation in generative AI, especially in customer service, marketing, and e-commerce. Yet the same survey suggested that broad enterprise implementation remained rare. Public evidence for physical-world decisioning is thinner. Retailers appear to have no shortage of AI pilots. They have fewer publicly documented cases that show, in plain numbers, how store-side sensing, identity, and decisioning alter labor productivity or conversion at scale.[9]

The technology is real, but the commercial lesson is more modest. In the near term, the most credible use cases are queue management, inventory-aware service, omnichannel pickup execution, and known-customer clienteling. These are not glamorous. They do, however, have the advantage of already existing.[6][7][10][12]

## The store is getting instrumented

If the question is whether physical retail can be sensed in real time, the answer is yes. The question is how far that sensing can travel into individual commercial action.

Amazon’s Just Walk Out remains one of the clearest public demonstrations of serious sensor fusion in retail. Amazon says the system uses computer vision, machine learning, and shelf sensors to track what shoppers pick up or return and to automate checkout. It has described deployments in more than 70 Amazon-owned stores and more than 85 third-party locations across the United States, the United Kingdom, and Australia. At Lumen Field, Amazon said its first deployment more than doubled sales relative to the previous store in the same spot.[10]

That is impressive evidence of a highly instrumented retail environment. It is also evidence of something narrower than the “hidden high-intent customer” thesis. The commercial task here is friction reduction at checkout, not premium service allocation. Reuters reported in 2024 that Amazon was extending the technology to more third-party stores while reducing its own reliance on it in Fresh grocery stores, and cited issues including missed items, delayed receipts, customer confusion, and human reviewers used to improve performance. The case proves that sensor fusion can work commercially. It does not prove that the average retailer can turn store sensing into elegant, customer-level intervention across formats.[11]

The more plausible near-term shift is visible in apparel. Gap’s 2025 rollout with RADAR uses RFID, AI, and computer vision to help associates locate items, replenish stock, and fulfill omnichannel orders. The point is not that the store suddenly knows the customer. The point is that the store begins to know where the product is, where the exception is, and where labor may need to go next. In many categories, that may be the more important intelligence to build first.[12]

There is a reason this matters for fashion and specialty retail in particular. Stores in these sectors do not merely sell stock. They sell fit, comparison, reassurance, and speed. A customer who cannot find a size or who waits too long for help has not experienced a subtle model error. She has experienced bad retail. Inventory visibility, routeing, and timely service can therefore matter more than speculative intent scoring, at least in the next 12 to 24 months.[12]

## Identity is still the hinge

The weak link in the more ambitious thesis is not sensing alone. It is identity.

Online, a retailer can often connect browsing history, saved products, loyalty status, purchase history, and current session behavior. In store, that continuity is far more conditional. Adobe’s customer profile and identity documentation makes the logic clear. Profiles become useful when known identifiers can be stitched together. If that stitching has not yet happened, even online personalization may not update instantly. In a store, where the window to act can be measured in seconds, this matters.[6][7]

The strongest evidence for in-store identity therefore comes from customers who have already chosen to be known. Nike’s membership ecosystem links app behavior and store services. Sephora’s Beauty Insider model allows members to view both online and in-store purchase history when transactions are linked to the account. Luxury clienteling systems do something similar, though usually with more human mediation. Zegna said in 2023 that Zegna X, developed with Microsoft, accounted for more than 45 percent of boutique revenues in geographies where travel and movement had normalized. That is not proof of ambient store intelligence. It is company-reported evidence that known-customer augmentation can be commercially meaningful when the relationship is already there.[13][14][15]

This is why the stronger claim is narrower. Retailers can do much more when a shopper logs in, checks in, scans a member credential, books an appointment, uses buy online pick up in store, or otherwise declares herself. They can do much less, at least with confidence, when she does not.

There is also a privacy problem, and it is not a small one. FTC guidance has long documented how location and device signals can be used in retail tracking. Apple and Android have both described MAC randomization features that make passive device tracking less straightforward. The FTC’s action against InMarket showed that regulators are willing to act when precise location data is used without adequate consent. The commercial implication is straightforward. Retailers should build selective identity, not universal surveillance. The former is commercially more usable. The latter is more likely to create regulatory, reputational, and customer-trust problems.[16][17][18][19]

This is the point at which many conversations about “high intent” become fuzzy. Dwell, revisits, and product interaction may indicate purchase readiness. They may also indicate confusion, indecision, or a store design failure. Public evidence for reliable, cross-format intent detection in live retail environments remains thin. Exploratory research exists, including a 2023 SSRN working paper using in-store video and transaction data to predict purchase behavior in a large Asian retail chain. But working papers are not operating models, and they do not erase the governance questions that come with trying to infer too much from behavior in physical space.[20]

## The next operating advantage is more prosaic

So what does this mean for the next 12 to 24 months? It means the store is more likely to become event-driven before it becomes fully agentic.

That may sound less dramatic than the original thesis, but it is also more useful. A store that can detect queue formation, BOPIS arrival, fitting-room demand, product-location exceptions, and known-customer service moments can already allocate labor more intelligently than one relying on schedule grids and manager intuition. If labor is scarce, the economic goal is not to personalize every interaction. It is to direct scarce human attention where expected value is highest.[4][5][12]

For grocery, big-box, and store-led omnichannel chains, that likely means better orchestration around fulfillment, queueing, and service recovery. For fashion and specialty retail, it means faster item finding, stronger associate prompts, and better matching of known customers to the right service moments. For luxury, it means expanding clienteling systems that enrich associate judgment rather than replacing it. In each case, the store can become smarter by becoming easier to interpret and manage in real time.[12][15]

The architecture behind this is easy to describe and hard to build. Retailers need a session layer that can combine what the store senses, what the customer has consented to share, what inventory is available, what labor is available, and which decisions should remain human. The hard problem is not the language model. It is the orchestration of systems and permissions around real commercial moments.[6][7][9]

This is also where power begins to move. As more commerce decisions move through software, merchants will need cleaner product data, clearer policies, and more reliable inventory. But physical retail still has one advantage digital channels do not: the ability to improve the handoff between product, place, and person. That advantage only holds if the store stops operating half blind.

What matters next is not the volume of AI talk, but whether retailers begin producing harder store-side evidence: faster service response, higher revenue per labor hour, better item-find rates, tighter omnichannel execution, and stronger conversion from known-customer interventions. If those numbers start showing up in public, the hidden high-intent customer will stop being a compelling thought experiment and start becoming an operating reality. Until then, the more plausible near-term shift is simpler. Retailers are not building sentient stores. They are building stores that can finally see enough to act.
