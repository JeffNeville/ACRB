# The Coming War Over Returns

**Deck:** Returns are already a costly contest over margin, fraud, and customer trust; the next shift is not fully autonomous shopping agents, but a machine-mediated struggle among retailer policy engines, payment networks, and increasingly capable consumer-side assistants.

## The expensive afterlife of a sale

Retailers used to treat returns as a courtesy, then as a conversion tool. They now look more like a battleground. In 2023 the National Retail Federation estimated that American retailers took back $743 billion of goods, or 14.5% of sales.[1] The exact figure is less important than the shape of the problem. Returns are large, volatile, and especially punishing in categories such as apparel, where fit uncertainty, style mismatch, and deliberate multi-size ordering keep rates structurally high.[1][2][3]

The costs do not stop at postage. A returned item must be received, inspected, graded, repackaged, rerouted, discounted, liquidated, or discarded. Reverse logistics is inherently messier than forward distribution because the condition and resale value of each item are uncertain.[4][5] In fashion, where value decays with seasonality and handling, time is money twice over.[3][4]

That has changed the tone of retail policy. Over the past two years many merchants have shortened return windows, imposed fees for mailed returns, excluded more categories, and steered shoppers toward cheaper channels such as store drop-off or box-free consolidation.[6][7][8] Amazon, Zara, H&M, J.Crew, and Boohoo have all adjusted return terms in ways that make the economics more explicit.[6][8][9] The era of blanket leniency is giving way to selective tolerance.

This is often framed as a customer-service issue. It is not. Returns now sit at the junction of logistics, fraud, payments, and compliance. A single order can generate a return request, a customer-service claim, and a card dispute over the same transaction.[10][11] The sale is no longer complete at checkout. It enters a period of adjudication.

## From discretion to rules

The most important change is not that artificial intelligence is taking over returns. It is that retailers are turning a discretionary process into a codified one.

That shift is already visible. Return eligibility is increasingly defined by SKU, channel, geography, timing, condition, and customer segment.[6][8] Retailers also use analytics to distinguish between low-risk and high-risk returners. The best-known example is The Retail Equation, whose systems help merchants assess return risk using transaction and behavior data.[12] Public reporting and litigation show the controversy this can create, especially when denials appear opaque to consumers.[13][14] Still, the underlying point is clear: returns are no longer governed only by store associates and call-center scripts. They are increasingly filtered through policy engines.

This codification reflects hard economics. Academic research has long shown that generous return policies can raise purchase incidence and trust, but also invite adverse selection and abuse.[15][16] The trade-off is sharper online, and sharper still in apparel, where bracketing has become a normalized response to poor sizing consistency.[2][17] A retailer that promises frictionless returns may gain conversion while quietly subsidizing costly behavior.

Machine-readable rules are the natural response. They reduce inconsistency across channels. They make it easier to route low-value items to returnless refunds when shipping and processing cost more than recovery is worth.[8][18] They also create the basis for tighter fraud controls and cleaner evidence when a customer bypasses the return flow and files a chargeback instead.[10][11]

This matters because the next contest in returns will not be fought mainly by humans. It will be fought by systems. On one side sit retailer policy engines, fraud models, and dispute-management tools. On another sit card-network rules and payment processors that increasingly automate evidence gathering and representment.[10][11][19][20] On the third, still emerging side, sit consumer-facing assistants that can interpret policies, draft requests, and eventually act on a shopper’s behalf.[21][22][23]

The idea of a war over returns is broadly right. The evidence suggests a narrower and more useful version. The conflict is not yet between fully autonomous consumer agents and retailers at scale. It is between increasingly automated systems with different objectives. Retailers want to protect margin. Payment networks want standardized evidence and process. Consumers, or their software, want the path of least friction.

## The machine on the customer’s side

There is credible evidence that AI is spreading through retail customer service and post-purchase support. Enterprise software firms such as Salesforce and Zendesk now market AI agents that can handle parts of service workflows within guardrails.[24][25] IBM and Salesforce surveys suggest broad interest in AI for customer service and retail operations, though such surveys are better at capturing intent than proving impact.[26][27] Shopify, Amazon, and Zalando have all discussed or launched AI features across commerce and customer experience.[22][28][29]

Yet the leap from AI-assisted service to autonomous consumer-side returns is still mostly a forecast. Public evidence that shoppers are already using agents at meaningful scale to initiate returns, choose dispute pathways, or exploit policy loopholes is thin.[21][22][23] There is even less evidence that major retailers have redesigned returns operations specifically because of such agents.

That distinction matters. It is easy to mistake technical possibility for operating reality. A chatbot that explains a return policy is not the same thing as an external agent with delegated authority to trigger a refund. A merchant-side assistant that drafts a response is not the same thing as a consumer-side system that compares return, exchange, resale, and chargeback options to maximize utility.

Still, the direction is clear enough to justify preparation. Platform companies say they are building agents that can complete tasks, not just answer questions.[21][23] Browser and commerce platforms are experimenting with more delegated task flows.[30] If those capabilities mature, post-purchase actions will become a natural frontier. Returns are rule-bound, repetitive, and economically consequential. Such tasks suit software well.

The strategic implication is not that retailers should panic about armies of rogue bots. It is that ambiguity will become expensive. A human customer can tolerate a vague policy page and a bit of improvisation from customer service. A machine will either fail, escalate, or probe for loopholes. The more interactions are machine-mediated, the more policy must be explicit, structured, and auditable.

## Fraud, disputes, and the blurred boundary of a return

Returns abuse is one reason this matters. NRF and Appriss Retail estimated that fraudulent and abusive returns cost American retailers $101 billion in 2023.[31] As with most industry estimates, the number should be treated cautiously. The phenomenon is better established than the precision of the total. Fraud vectors include wardrobing, receipt fraud, item switching, empty-box claims, and false assertions that an item never arrived.[10][31][32]

The more interesting development is that returns abuse increasingly overlaps with payments. Visa and Mastercard have formal dispute reason codes, evidence standards, and merchant processes for contesting chargebacks.[10][11] Providers such as Stripe and Adyen offer automated tools to assemble evidence and manage representment.[19][20] Visa’s Compelling Evidence 3.0 framework is designed to help merchants rebut some fraud claims using linked transaction and device data.[33]

This means a return is no longer just a reverse-logistics event. It is also a potential payments event. A customer who does not like the answer from a retailer’s returns portal may try customer service, then a chargeback. A future consumer agent could do the same, only faster and with less patience. It could compare the expected payoff from a standard return, an exchange, a partial refund request, a returnless concession, or a card dispute. That is plausible economics, even if it is not yet proven market behavior.

Retailers therefore face a problem of organizational design as much as technology. Many still split responsibility for returns across customer service, stores, logistics, finance, fraud, and e-commerce. That fragmentation was inefficient when the process was mostly manual. It becomes dangerous when the same customer event can ricochet across systems that do not share rules or signals.[4][10][33]

The firms best positioned for the next phase will treat post-purchase as a single control surface. They will connect return eligibility, identity, payment credentials, dispute history, and recovery economics. They will know when a mailed return should be discouraged, when a store drop-off should be nudged, when a low-value item should be refunded without return, and when a claim should be contested rather than conceded.[8][18][33] None of this requires science fiction. It requires coherent systems.

## The hard question of delegated authority

The most immediate executive question is not whether AI will transform returns. It is whether retailers should allow software to act without asking the customer again.

That question already has analogues in payments and data regulation. Rules on automated decision-making, authentication, and delegated access do not directly answer the question for retail returns, but they suggest a preference for clear authority, transparency, and recourse.[34][35][36][37] A return that triggers a refund, inventory write-down, or payment reversal is not a trivial click. It is a financial action.

The sensible answer is likely to be conditional, not absolute. Low-risk information actions can probably be delegated. An agent should be able to retrieve order data, check eligibility, generate a label, or locate a drop-off point. Workflow actions are more delicate. Initiating a return request or scheduling a pickup may be acceptable under standing consent, meaning pre-authorized by the customer, in some cases. Financial actions should face stronger controls. Changing the refund method, waiving fees, issuing store credit, or escalating to a dispute should usually require step-up confirmation, especially for higher-value items or higher-risk customers.[35][37]

That may sound obvious. Many retailers are not set up to enforce it cleanly. Their policies are scattered across websites, call-center scripts, store manuals, and hard-coded platform logic. Their logs may show that a return happened, but not whether it was initiated by a customer, a service agent, or a delegated system. If a shopper later says, “my assistant did that, not me,” the retailer will need a clear record.

This is where governance becomes commercial. Algorithmic denials already create backlash when customers cannot understand or appeal them.[13][14] The same will apply to delegated actions. If retailers permit external agents to act, they will need records of consent, action history, and override rights. If they do not permit it, they will need systems that can distinguish between assistance and execution. Either way, the issue is not futuristic. It is a design choice.

## Not every return is abuse

There is a risk in all this that retailers overlearn the wrong lesson. Tightening controls can protect margin, but it can also punish legitimate customers and mask deeper merchandising failures.

Apparel is the clearest example. High return rates often reflect fit uncertainty, inconsistent sizing, weak product data, and merchandising practices that encourage over-ordering.[2][17][38] Better sizing tools, richer product information, and improved recommendation systems can reduce mismatch-related returns.[38][39][40] If consumer-side AI gets better at matching shoppers to products before purchase, it could lower returns rather than raise them. The evidence does not yet show which effect will dominate.[15][16][38] Technology is unlikely to eliminate the tactile and embodied elements of apparel shopping.

That uncertainty is important. It means the coming contest over returns is not simply a matter of defense. Retailers can respond by hardening policy, but they should also improve the upstream data that makes returns less likely in the first place. Prevention may still beat adjudication.

The same caution applies to fraud metrics. Industry reports often bundle together outright fraud, policy abuse, and rational consumer responses to poor fit or poor information.[31] Those are not the same problem. One calls for enforcement. Another calls for better product data. A third may call for a different commercial bargain altogether.

The strongest retailers will separate those cases rather than treating every return as a moral failing. They will also resist the temptation to believe vendor marketing about autonomous service. The evidence today supports a more prosaic but more urgent conclusion. Returns are already a high-stakes optimization problem. Automation is already changing how they are interpreted, routed, and contested. Payment disputes are already part of the same system. Consumer agents are coming, but their scale and authority remain unsettled.

That is enough to justify action. The firms that fare best will not be those with the flashiest chatbot. They will be those that turn post-purchase into governed infrastructure: explicit rules, shared signals, clear consent, and auditable decisions. In retail, the sale has always been the glamorous moment. The next competitive edge may lie in what happens after the customer changes their mind.
