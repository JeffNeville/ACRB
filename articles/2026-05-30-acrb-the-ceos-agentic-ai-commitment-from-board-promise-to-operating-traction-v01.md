# The CEO’s Agentic AI Commitment: From Board Promise to Operating Traction

**Deck:** Retail bosses do not need a grand theory of agentic AI. They need a disciplined way to tell the board where value will appear, where risk will sit, and why governance is the thing that lets the firm move faster.

## The promise, and the problem

Hype is cheap. Operating traction is not.

Retail boards are now hearing a familiar proposition: agentic AI will change everything. The trouble is that the phrase is doing too much work. It is used to describe tools that draft product copy, systems that route customer-service tickets, software that can call other software, and visions of semi-autonomous commercial operations. Those are not the same thing. Treating them as one category is a good way to confuse a board and misallocate capital.[1]

A narrower and more useful view is to treat agentic AI not as a single enterprise capability, but as a portfolio of AI-enabled functions with different levels of maturity, risk, and economic value.[1][2] Some are already useful in bounded workflows. Some are promising but immature. Some remain mostly a marketing claim. Gartner’s public framing is clear on this point: agentic AI is a set of innovations moving at different speeds, not one coherent technology wave.[1]

That matters because retail is especially vulnerable to inflated claims. The industry is full of repetitive work, thin margins, messy data, and costly exceptions. It is easy to imagine agents fixing all of it. It is much harder to show, in production, that they can do so reliably. Public evidence for autonomous multi-agent systems remaking core retail operations at scale in the near term remains limited.[1][3] What does emerge more clearly is something more prosaic and more valuable: a disciplined operating model for deploying AI in a few high-friction workflows where the data is usable, the decisions are repeatable, and the economics can be measured.[2][4][5]

That is the board conversation worth having. Not “Are we doing agentic AI?” but “Which workflows are being improved, what rights are being delegated to machines, what controls are in place, and how will the firm know whether any of this is producing real value?”[2][6]

## A portfolio, not a manifesto

The old corporate habit in technology is to announce a transformation before deciding what is being transformed. Agentic AI invites exactly that mistake. A retail chief executive who presents it as a singular strategic bet is likely to promise too much, too early. A better approach is to classify the work.

A useful distinction is between assistive systems, semi-autonomous systems, and broad autonomy claims.[1][3] Assistive systems help humans inside bounded workflows. Semi-autonomous systems can execute predefined tasks under policy constraints and escalation rules. Broad autonomy implies much wider decision-making authority across complex processes. The first category is already credible in several domains. The second is emerging. The third remains weakly evidenced in retail.[1][2][3]

This is more than semantics. It is the difference between a board promise that can be governed and one that cannot. Many products sold as agents are really combinations of large language models, retrieval tools, workflow software, and rules engines.[7] That may be perfectly useful. But it means the right test is not whether the software looks agentic in a demo. It is whether it improves a workflow’s speed, quality, control, or cost in live operations.

In retail, several near-term candidates recur in public disclosures and industry research. The research points to customer-service assistance, product-data enrichment, marketing-content operations, forecasting support, replenishment exception handling, and compliance review as plausible domains for early value.[3][5][8][9] These are not glamorous. They are bounded, repetitive, and measurable. That is precisely why they matter.

The board narrative should therefore be built around a portfolio. One part of that portfolio should aim to strengthen proprietary advantage, where the retailer’s own data, judgment, brand, or supplier relationships matter. Another part should lower the marginal cost of commoditized work. Research consistently points to the importance of proprietary data, workflow integration, and complementary assets in AI value capture.[2][10][11] It does not prove that every retailer should draw the line in the same place. Product copy may be generic for a marketplace seller and strategic for a luxury house. Customer service may be a cost center for one chain and a relationship asset for another.[10][11]

That is why the useful question is not whether a use case is “AI-worthy.” It is whether it sits in the part of the business where better judgment or lower cost actually changes the economics.

## Where the evidence is strongest

The best evidence for AI at work still comes from bounded environments. Customer service is the clearest example. Research by Erik Brynjolfsson, Danielle Li, and Lindsey Raymond found that generative AI assistance improved productivity in a large customer-support setting, with the biggest gains accruing to less experienced workers.[12] The study was not about modern agent orchestration, but its lesson is highly relevant. AI can improve performance when the workflow is high-volume, text-rich, and naturally supervised.

That pattern travels well into retail. Many service operations have clear workflows, historical data, measurable indicators, and defined escalation paths.[5][8][13] It is therefore reasonable for a retailer to start there. What is not yet reasonable is to assume that service agents can broadly resolve end-to-end issues across refunds, order changes, loyalty adjustments, and policy exceptions without creating new operational risk. Public evidence for scaled, low-risk autonomy in such settings remains limited.[8][13][14]

Product-data and content operations are another promising domain. Retailers have long struggled with inconsistent attributes, localization, image tagging, and taxonomy management. Generative AI is well suited to high-volume, modular work of this kind, especially when outputs can be checked against structured rules or human review.[5][9][15][16] The attraction is not only technical. It is economic. Product content is repetitive, multilingual, and often slow. Faster throughput can improve time to market and reduce manual effort. Yet even here, the strategic value varies. For some firms, this is industrial back-office work. For others, especially premium brands, product storytelling and brand voice are part of the offer itself.[10][16]

Planning and replenishment sit in a more ambiguous category. The logic for AI support is strong. Retail planning is full of exceptions, anomalies, and cross-functional handoffs. Systems that explain forecast deviations, summarize supplier risks, recommend actions, or flag policy conflicts could be useful.[3][17] But the public evidence is thin. Retailers have disclosed AI use in forecasting and inventory optimization for years, yet those disclosures rarely isolate the contribution of newer generative or agentic techniques.[17] Improvements are also hard to attribute cleanly because they are often entangled with process redesign, better data, and changing demand conditions. This is a domain of active experimentation, not settled proof.

Marketing operations are similar. Research points to productivity gains in content drafting, adaptation, and localization.[15][16] In retail, where campaigns must be translated across channels and geographies, that can reduce cycle time and external spend. But the strategic value is uneven. AI can lower the cost of repetitive adaptation work. It is less clear that it can create durable creative advantage.[10][16]

The pattern is consistent across these examples. The nearer the workflow is to structured data, repeatable tasks, measurable outputs, and human review, the stronger the case. The farther it moves toward tacit judgment, cross-functional ambiguity, and consequential exceptions, the weaker the case becomes.

## The real bottleneck is not the model

This is where the Gartner hype-cycle story meets the McKinsey operating-model story. The limiting factor in enterprise AI is rarely access to a model. It is the ability to deploy one inside a workflow that has clean data, clear ownership, usable controls, and a path to financial impact.[2][4][18]

That finding is remarkably consistent across enterprise research. Organizations that scale AI tend to focus on a small number of high-value domains, align business and technology teams, redesign workflows, invest in adoption, and build governance into deployment rather than bolting it on later.[2][4][18] They do not win by running the most pilots. They win by converting a few pilots into operating routines.

Retail has particular reasons to take this seriously. Product, inventory, pricing, customer, and supplier data are often scattered across systems and geographies. Process ownership is fragmented. Policies are unevenly codified. In such conditions, an agent is only as good as the context it can access and the systems it can safely touch.[6][18][19] A reasoning model without current inventory visibility or promotion rules is not a strategist. It is a confident guesser. A content generator without approved brand standards and legal constraints may increase rework rather than reduce it.[5][19]

This is why governance should be seen less as a brake than as steering and suspension. The phrase McKinsey likes, that governance is the brakes that enable speed, is directionally right.[2] But only if the controls are reusable and embedded. If every use case requires bespoke review, governance becomes theater. If standards for data access, model risk, human oversight, auditability, and escalation are built into the operating system, teams can move faster because they know the boundaries.[2][6][20][21]

The regulatory direction reinforces this. The EU AI Act and NIST’s AI Risk Management Framework both stress governance, accountability, transparency, and human oversight for consequential uses of AI.[20][21] Retail is not the most heavily regulated sector, but many of its attractive use cases touch pricing, employment, fraud, customer treatment, and compliance. Those are not domains in which a chief executive should be vague about decision rights.

A practical way to frame this is simple: what can the system observe, what can it recommend, what can it execute, what requires approval, and what triggers escalation?[6][20][21] That is a much better board question than whether humans are “in the loop” in some abstract sense.

## What boards should ask, and what CEOs should avoid saying

The temptation in boardrooms is to ask for a grand strategy. The better demand is for a credible portfolio. A practical framework is to sort use cases by strategic value and by process-data readiness.[2][4][18] High-value, high-readiness domains are the places to deploy now. High-value, low-readiness domains require foundational work first. Low-value, high-readiness domains are candidates for efficient industrialization. Low-value, low-readiness domains should usually be deferred, outsourced, or ignored.

This is not a canonical industry matrix. It is a synthesis. But it is grounded in the research and has the virtue of forcing two disciplines at once: strategic choice and operational honesty.[2][4]

An overlay can make it more useful. Retail leaders can think in terms of four broad agent patterns: deep reasoners for context-heavy analysis, content workers for repeatable language and image tasks, swarms for high-volume modular execution, and guardian agents for exceptions, outliers, and skeptical oversight. The taxonomy is not an industry standard and should not be presented as one. It is an analytical tool.[1][3] Its value is that it stops executives from talking about “agents” as if all machine work had the same risk profile.

The board, then, should expect management to answer four questions. Where can the company deploy now with confidence? Where is the value high but the foundations weak? Where should AI reinforce a distinctive capability? And where should it simply lower the cost of generic work?[2][10][11]

It should also expect management to avoid a few claims. One is that autonomy itself is the goal. The research suggests that measurable value matters more than full autonomy, and that humans remain essential in high-stakes or exception-heavy workflows.[1][2][20][21] Another is that pilot volume proves progress. It does not. Many firms run AI pilots without redesigning workflows, assigning business accountability, or translating productivity into P&L impact.[2][4][18] A third is that productivity gains are the same as financial gains. They are not.

That last point deserves more attention than it gets. Faster copy creation, quicker service handling, or reduced analyst time only matter if they become lower external spend, reduced overtime, higher throughput, faster time to market, better conversion, lower markdowns, or less shrink.[2][18] Otherwise the firm has produced activity, not value. Board reporting should therefore separate activity metrics from operational metrics and financial metrics. Prompts executed and users trained are not useless numbers. They are just not the numbers that matter most.

## The hard part is still management

There is a serious counterpoint to the current enthusiasm, and it should be taken seriously. Much of the public evidence in retail still comes from vendors, systems integrators, and company press releases.[5][9][13] These sources are useful for spotting where experimentation is happening. They are weak proof of durable impact. They often lack baseline metrics, control groups, time horizons, and full cost accounting. Survey enthusiasm also runs ahead of deployment evidence.[1][4]

That does not mean the field is empty. It means the burden of proof should remain high. Retail is full of edge cases, tacit judgment, and costly mistakes. Hallucinations, brittle reasoning, and context errors are not theoretical nuisances in such an environment. They are operating risks.[14][22][23] The right response is neither to dismiss the technology nor to surrender to it. It is to manage it as an operating portfolio with explicit decision rights, measurable value cases, and governance that is designed for reuse.

This leaves the chief executive with a more prosaic task. The job is not to declare an agentic future. It is to choose two or three domains where the economics are disproportionate, make business and technology one team, improve the data and process foundations, and insist that any productivity gain be translated into real commercial outcomes.[2][4][18] It is less glamorous than a manifesto and more useful than a burst of pilots.

The distinction matters because boards will soon ask for proof of operating results, not declarations of intent. The retailers with the clearest case will be those that can say, with precision, where machine judgment is useful, where human judgment remains indispensable, and how that mix is affecting the numbers that count.
