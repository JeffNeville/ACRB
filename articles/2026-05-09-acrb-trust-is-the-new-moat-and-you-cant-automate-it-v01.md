# Intelligence is Cheap, Trust is Expensive: The New Calculus of Agentic Commerce

**Deck:** Retailers do not yet face a world in which autonomous agents control vast swaths of demand. They do face one in which AI increasingly mediates discovery, recommendation and service, making trust an operating capability rather than a brand slogan.

## The trillion-dollar distraction

Agentic commerce has become a fashionable idea. Its champions talk of software agents that will search, compare, decide, and buy on consumers’ behalf. Its skeptics reply that this is mostly vapor, a confusion of chatbot traffic with actual delegated spending.[1] Both camps are arguing over the wrong thing.

The immediate question for retail is not whether machines can shop. In narrow ways, they already can. The more important question is whether consumers will trust them to do so, and under what conditions.[2] That is a less glamorous problem than autonomous purchasing. It is also the question for which there is at least some usable evidence.

The strongest data so far show a sharp rise in AI-generated referral traffic to retail sites, not a mass handover of purchasing authority. Adobe reported a 1,300% year-over-year increase in traffic to U.S. retail sites from generative-AI sources during the 2024 holiday season, with those visitors showing higher engagement than traffic from some other channels.[3] This matters. It suggests that AI is already becoming a meaningful layer in product discovery and decision support. But it does not show that consumers are broadly outsourcing consequential buying decisions to autonomous agents.

That distinction is not semantic. It is strategic. If AI is mainly mediating discovery, then the near-term contest is not over who builds the cleverest shopping bot. It is over which retailers can be represented accurately, safely, and persuasively when an AI system stands between brand and buyer. In that world, trust stops being a slogan. It becomes part of the operating model.[4]

This is a useful corrective to both the exuberance and the cynicism. The exuberant case often leaps from rising AI traffic to bullish revenue forecasts without proving that shoppers are willing to delegate meaningful authority.[1] The cynical case, including the argument that agentic commerce is a “collective hallucination,” is a fair warning against hype but risks missing the operational shift already underway.[1] Retailers are redesigning customer service, search, content production, forecasting, and internal workflows around AI.[5] The market may not be ready for autonomous shopping at scale. It is already moving toward AI-mediated commerce.

## Assistance is here; authority is not

Consumers are not indifferent to the difference between help and control. Across surveys and markets, they appear more comfortable with AI as an assistant than as an authority.[2] IBM’s consumer research finds shoppers using AI-enabled tools for product research, recommendations, and service, but with uneven adoption and clear sensitivity to trust.[2] KPMG and the University of Melbourne found widespread use of AI tools globally, but only limited willingness to trust them unconditionally, with concerns centered on misinformation, manipulation, and accountability.[6] Salesforce reports a similar pattern: customers like speed and convenience, but want disclosure and access to humans when needed.[7]

That pattern matters more in some categories than in others. In apparel, fit uncertainty, returns friction, and identity signaling remain central to the purchase decision.[8] In luxury, authenticity and provenance are part of the product, not an optional extra.[9] In beauty and wellness, trust is tied to safety, efficacy, and personal relevance.[10] These are not ideal conditions for blind delegation. A consumer may happily ask an AI to summarize reviews, compare options, or suggest a routine replenishment. Handing over broad authority to buy a dress, a serum, or a handbag is another matter.

This is why the most defensible version of the agentic-commerce thesis is narrower than the loudest forecasts suggest. The near-term battleground appears to be trusted delegation, not full autonomy.[2] Consumers may accept AI support for low-risk tasks before they accept broad autonomous purchasing. That is consistent with both survey evidence and basic consumer-behavior theory around perceived risk.[6]

Retailers should take this seriously precisely because it is modest. A technology does not need to be fully autonomous to reorder an industry. Search did not have to become sentient to reshape retail economics. If AI systems increasingly influence what consumers see, how products are described, and which options feel credible, then the firms that feed those systems clean, consistent, trustworthy information gain an advantage even before a single autonomous agent checks out a cart.[3][11]

## Trust is not a message. It is an operational capability.

Retailers often talk about trust as if it were a matter for marketing. The evidence points elsewhere. In digital commerce, trust is increasingly the product of system design, governance, and recourse.[4][12]

The World Economic Forum’s work on digital trust emphasizes security, transparency, interoperability, and accountability.[4] NIST’s AI Risk Management Framework defines trustworthy AI in terms such as validity, reliability, safety, privacy, explainability, and accountability.[12] The language is dry. They also happen to describe the practical conditions under which a retailer can survive AI mediation without confusing customers or embarrassing itself.

For commerce, this starts with information integrity. Product attributes, prices, availability, delivery promises, and return policies need to be accurate and machine-readable.[11][13] A human shopper can sometimes compensate for a muddled product page or a vague policy. An AI system is less forgiving. If the underlying data are inconsistent, the recommendation will be inconsistent too. If the policy logic is unclear, the service experience will fail at the first exception.

This is why the most important AI work in retail may be the least theatrical. Unified product information management. Better taxonomy. Real-time inventory visibility. Policy engines that can handle substitutions, returns, and exceptions. Audit trails for recommendations and decisions.[11][12][13] None of this is keynote material. All of it matters more than a polished chatbot sitting on top of brittle operations.

The same logic applies to governance. Retailers need controls over hallucinations, outdated claims, biased outputs, and policy noncompliance.[12][14] They also need clear escalation paths when automation fails. Research on service automation and service recovery is consistent on this point: customers tolerate automation far more readily when they can reach a human in complex, emotional, or exception-heavy situations.[15] The lesson from AI in customer service is not that human involvement has regained importance. It is that over-automation without recourse is a fast way to destroy confidence.

Klarna’s experience captures the tension. The company touted large productivity gains from its AI customer-service assistant, then later signaled a renewed emphasis on the quality of human support.[16] That is not a contradiction. It is a reminder that efficiency at the front end can create fragility at the back end. Delegated commerce will generate disputes: wrong item, poor fit, unauthorized substitution, missed delivery, counterfeit concerns. If the retailer cannot resolve those cleanly, the customer’s quarrel will not be with the algorithm alone. It will be with the brand.

## The categories that feel the strain first

Not every retail segment will experience this shift in the same way. In commodity categories, convenience and price may continue to dominate, and AI may simply compress search costs further.[2][7] In higher-consideration categories, trust is likely to be more binding.

Fashion is the obvious case. Online apparel has long been shaped by uncertainty over fit and by the cost of returns.[8] AI can help narrow choices, suggest outfits, and summarize reviews. It cannot abolish the fact that clothing is both functional and expressive. The same is true, in a different department, for beauty and wellness, where advice is personal and mistakes can feel riskier than a bad purchase in a low-stakes category.[10]

Luxury is even more revealing. There, authenticity, provenance, and clienteling are not peripheral. They are central to willingness to pay.[9][17] That helps explain the interest in traceability initiatives such as the Aura Blockchain Consortium, backed by LVMH, Prada Group, and Cartier.[18] The commercial impact of such systems remains uneven, but the strategic logic is plain enough. If digital intermediaries, including AI systems, increasingly shape discovery and recommendation, then verifiable trust signals become more valuable.

Resale offers a harsher version of the same lesson. Platforms such as The RealReal have built their proposition around authentication, yet the category has also faced disputes and litigation over authenticity claims.[19] That does not show that trust infrastructure is futile. It shows that it is hard, expensive, and commercially decisive. In categories where value depends on authenticity, trust cannot be improvised.

There is a broader implication here. The original, more ambitious claim that the winners in the agentic era will be brands whose identity is clear enough for an agent to represent faithfully is plausible, but not yet proven.[20] There is strong theory behind it. Brand clarity should help any intermediary, human or machine, summarize a firm coherently. But the evidence today supports a more practical claim: retailers with cleaner data, clearer policies, stronger provenance signals, and more coherent brand representation may be easier for AI systems to interpret and less likely to be misrepresented.[11][20]

That is not quite the same thing as saying trust is a moat. In retail, trust is often contingent and reversible. A better phrase might be that trust is becoming a necessary operating capability. Firms without it will struggle to compete in AI-mediated environments. Firms with it are not guaranteed victory, but they at least earn the right to be represented accurately.

## The counterpoint: convenience still wins, often

There is a risk of romanticizing this turn toward trust and human connection. Consumers are perfectly capable of trading warmth for convenience, especially in low-risk, repeatable tasks.[2][7] The evidence does not support a broad return to human-centric retail. It supports selective humanization.

That matters because some of the rhetoric around trust can become a refuge for incumbents. If agentic commerce is overhyped, one tempting response is to conclude that the old rules still apply and that brand heritage or store theater will do the job. They will not. AI may not yet be buying at scale, but it is already changing how products are found, compared, and explained.[3][5] Retailers that dismiss this because full autonomy is premature may discover that they have lost visibility long before they lose transactions.

There is also a serious structural counterpoint. Large marketplaces may be better positioned than many individual brands to provide machine-readable trust at scale. They already centralize reviews, seller policies, fulfillment standards, and identity systems.[21] Their problem is that they also centralize distrust: counterfeits, seller-quality variance, and opaque ranking systems remain persistent weaknesses.[22] The evidence suggests potential advantage, not inevitability.

This is where the debate becomes more interesting than the slogans. The advantage may not lie simply with the brands that deploy the best AI, nor with those with the warmest human story. It may belong to the firms, platforms, and ecosystems that can make trust legible to machines without making the customer feel trapped by them.

## What CEOs should watch next

For chief executives, the practical question is not whether to bet on a speculative trillion-dollar forecast. It is whether the company can support machine-readable trust.

That begins with a blunt audit. Is product and policy data reliable enough that an external AI system could represent the assortment without inventing details or mangling exceptions?[11][13] Are there clear decision rights over what automation may say or do in categories where fit, provenance, efficacy, or substitution are sensitive?[12][14] Is there a recourse process that customers can understand and use when AI gets it wrong?[15] And does anyone in the organization actually own these questions across merchandising, digital, legal, risk, customer care, and brand?

The firms that answer these questions well will not necessarily look the most futuristic. Their advantage may lie in being less brittle. They will be easier for AI systems to interpret, safer for customers to rely on, and quicker to recover when automation fails. That is a more sober ambition than the grandest agentic-commerce visions. It is also more likely to matter over the next few years.

The larger point is that retail is entering a phase in which intelligence is becoming cheaper, while trust remains stubbornly expensive. Models will improve. Interfaces will multiply. Some forms of delegated purchasing will spread, probably first in routine, low-risk categories.[2][6] The harder problem will persist: getting consumers to believe that a machine, a merchant, and the process connecting them can act without causing regret.

That is why trust deserves to be treated not as nostalgia, nor as a line in a brand book, but as competitive infrastructure. The companies that continuously reinforce customer trust will be prepared if autonomous commerce arrives more slowly than boosters hope, and if it arrives faster than skeptics expect. In retail, that is the prudent bet.
