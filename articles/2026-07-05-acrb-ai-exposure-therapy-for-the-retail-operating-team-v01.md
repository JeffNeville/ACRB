# AI Exposure Therapy for the Retail Operating Team

Kicker: Making AI ordinary

**Deck:** Retailers do not have an AI access problem so much as an operating-routine problem, and the firms that scale adoption are likely to be the ones that make AI a normal part of how decisions are prepared, reviewed, and challenged.

## The access trap

Management theory has long had a weakness for habits. The attraction is obvious enough. Grand intentions are cheap; repeated behavior is what changes institutions. Retailers now face a version of that problem with artificial intelligence. Many have bought licenses, opened access, run pilots, and held training sessions. Yet broad operating adoption remains patchy. The tools are present. The routines often are not.[1][2]

That gap is not unique to retail. Cross-industry surveys show a familiar pattern: generative AI use has risen quickly, but fewer firms report meaningful enterprise-wide effects than report experimentation or adoption.[1][2] Leaders are investing, employees are experimenting, and organizations are still struggling to turn scattered use into repeatable operating practice.[1][2]

The original temptation is to treat this as a training and Human Resource problem. If only merchants, planners, marketers, store operators, and finance teams were trained a bit more thoroughly, adoption would follow. The evidence suggests a narrower and more useful conclusion. Training matters, but generic training is not enough. Adoption is more likely when tools are embedded in work itself: in the recurring meetings, templates, reviews, and exception processes that govern how a retailer actually runs.[3][4]

That is where the metaphor of “exposure therapy” earns its keep, provided it is handled carefully. This is not a clinical model, and there is no evidence that therapy-style interventions have been validated for retail AI adoption. As a management metaphor, though, it captures something real. Many employees do not resist AI because they have studied it and rejected it on principle. A common risk is that they do not know where it fits, do not trust it enough, fear getting caught using it badly, or assume it belongs to the technical organization.[2][5][6] The practical answer is not to demand enthusiasm. It is to create repeated, low-risk, role-relevant encounters with AI inside normal business cadence until its use becomes ordinary.

That is a less glamorous ambition than “AI transformation.” It is also closer to what the evidence supports.

## From pilots to routines

Retail is especially prone to confusing access with adoption because so much of its work is cyclical. The business runs on rituals: line reviews, open-to-buy meetings, markdown discussions, campaign planning, labor scheduling, supplier negotiations, and weekly trade reviews. These routines are where decisions are made, defended, and revised. If AI does not appear there, it has not really entered the operating model.

Research on technology adoption has made this point for decades. People use new tools when they are useful in context, easy enough to apply, socially sanctioned, and supported by the surrounding system.[3][4] Recent work on enterprise AI says much the same in newer language. The benefits appear to depend not just on isolated models, but also on workflow redesign, organizational learning, and management systems that routinize use.[1][2]

For retailers, this distinction matters because the most visible AI activity often sits at the edge of the operating core. Customer-service copilots, content generation, internal knowledge assistants, and software-development aids are all real and increasingly common.[1][2] They are also easier to sandbox. A product-description draft can be reviewed before publication. A service summary can be checked by an agent. A knowledge assistant can answer policy questions without being handed authority over inventory or margin.

The harder question is what happens closer to commercial judgment. Can AI help prepare an assortment review, summarize demand anomalies, draft scenarios for a pricing discussion, or reduce the manual burden of weekly business reviews? Evidence suggests that this is where experimentation is moving. Yet public proof of scaled success remains limited.

That should not obscure the strategic point. The near-term opportunity is not full autonomy. It is assisted analysis. AI can surface exceptions faster, generate first-draft narratives, enable natural-language access to business intelligence, and help teams assemble recurring decision materials with less drudgery. In other words, it can become a standard layer in management preparation before it becomes a trusted decision-maker.

This is a more modest claim than some of the market’s “agentic” rhetoric. It is also more believable.

## Why people hesitate

Behavioral avoidance matters, but it is not the only explanation. Retailers face plenty of structural obstacles too: fragmented systems, weak data quality, unclear return on investment in some use cases, legal and compliance concerns, and the simple fact that many frontline roles have little spare time for experimentation.[2][5]

Still, the behavioral frictions are hard to dismiss. Trust, role ambiguity, and fear of error recur across the research. Executives worry about privacy, hallucinations, governance, and workforce readiness.[2][5] Employees often lack clarity on what is permitted, what is useful, and what will make them look careless rather than capable.[2][6] In many firms, AI arrives through IT, digital, or innovation teams, which can unintentionally signal that it is a specialist instrument rather than a general management aid.[2]

Retail adds its own complications. Internally, a visibly wrong recommendation on pricing, allocation, or labor can do more than produce a bad output. It can poison confidence in the tool itself. Research on algorithm aversion shows that people often underuse algorithmic advice after seeing it err, even when the system performs well on average.[7] That finding should make CEOs wary of a crude “deploy it everywhere” strategy. Repeated exposure can normalize use, but poor early exposure can entrench skepticism. Retailers also have an old and sensible constraint: AI use should not compromise the customer experience or trust in the brand.

This is one reason the strongest retail AI use cases today are still relatively narrow. Established applications such as forecasting, recommendations, pricing, fraud detection, and service automation have been in the sector for years. Generative AI has accelerated activity in customer service, marketing content, internal knowledge retrieval, and employee assistance.[1][2] These are useful proving grounds because outputs are inspectable and the downside of a bad first draft is usually lower than the downside of a bad inventory call.

The implication is subtle but important. “Make AI ordinary” does not mean “make it ubiquitous at once.” It means choosing operating moments where the tool is relevant, the answer can be checked, and the business risk is proportionate. In retail, that often starts with preparation rather than execution.

## The CEO’s real job

If the problem is not simply access, the CEO’s task changes. It becomes less about sponsoring another capability program and more about redesigning the company’s operating cadence.

The evidence points to a dual model. Governance, security, architecture, and model standards need central control. Use cases and workflow redesign need business ownership.[2][5] If AI adoption remains the responsibility of IT or an innovation office, operating adoption is likely to lag. Merchandising, planning, marketing, store operations, finance, and HR each need a small number of sanctioned AI moments in their own routines.[3][4]

That phrase, “sanctioned AI moments,” matters. Employees are more likely to use a tool when they know where it belongs, what standard applies, and who remains accountable. A retailer does not need every merchant to become a prompt engineer. It does need clarity on where AI may prepare, where it may recommend, and where it may act.[5] Drafting a weekly summary is one thing. Recommending markdown depth is another. Changing labor allocation automatically is something else again.

This is not only a compliance issue. It is an operating-model issue. As AI enters recurring decisions, firms will need explicit decision-rights frameworks based on business risk and reversibility.[5] The most practical distinction is simple enough: AI as preparer for summaries and first drafts; AI as recommender for scenarios and exceptions; AI as actor only in bounded, reversible, low-risk workflows. This is best understood as a practical operating framework for managing use.[5]

The management system must then reinforce the behavior. If executives ask for AI-assisted pre-reads, compare AI-generated scenarios with human judgment, and require teams to note where AI was used or rejected, they signal that use is legitimate, expected, and reviewable. If AI appears only in innovation showcases or vendor demos, it will remain ornamental.

There is a harder truth here. A common risk is that companies add AI on top of existing reporting burdens rather than removing work. That is a recipe for cynicism. If teams still produce every old deck manually and then are asked to “use AI too,” the technology becomes another management hobby. Firms are more likely to benefit when AI absorbs some of the preparation load and the routine is redesigned around that fact.

## What is real now, and what is not

The market’s language has moved faster than its operating reality. “Agentic” systems are now sold as if autonomous retail management were just around the corner. The evidence does not support that view. Systems that can plan and execute multi-step tasks are improving quickly, but reliability limitations, evaluation challenges, and the need for human oversight remain substantial.[5] In retail, many so-called agents are better understood as orchestrated assistants or workflow automations with a language interface.

That does not make them trivial. It simply places them in the right category. Near-term change is likely to come from AI becoming a routine participant in management preparation: synthesizing data, drafting narratives, surfacing anomalies, and documenting follow-through. That is plausible and adjacent to current enterprise copilots. What remains unproven is whether such use materially improves decision quality rather than merely reducing preparation time.[1][2]

A second plausible shift is that decision rights will become more explicitly tiered by AI confidence and business risk.[5] Retailers already know how to apply different controls to different decisions. AI will force them to formalize those distinctions more clearly. What is not yet well evidenced is how many retailers have done this at scale.

A third plausible shift is role redesign. Merchants, planners, and marketers may spend less time assembling analysis and more time judging options, exceptions, and trade-offs. Experimental evidence from knowledge work suggests that generative AI can improve speed and sometimes quality in drafting and analytical tasks.[8][9] In retail, that could free time for negotiation, prioritization, and cross-functional judgment. Yet history offers a warning. Companies often layer new tools onto old work rather than removing the old work itself. Productivity gains on paper do not automatically become better jobs or better decisions.

The biggest unresolved question is whether repeated use will build trust or merely expose flaws. The answer will depend on sequencing. Early use cases need to be inspectable, grounded in trusted enterprise context, and integrated with the systems people already use. Generic chat tools, detached from internal data and business rules, are unlikely to drive deep operating adoption on their own. Operators are right to distrust outputs that are generic, stale, or impossible to trace.

This is where architecture meets behavior. The more AI can access governed internal documents, metrics, prior decisions, and workflow systems, the more useful and credible it becomes. Without that context layer, the exhortation to “use AI” is little more than a slogan.

## Make it boring

Retailers like novelty less than they admit. Their best systems are repetitive by design. A good operating model turns judgment into disciplined routine without eliminating judgment altogether. AI adoption will probably follow the same logic.

The strongest evidence does not support a grand theory that behavioral avoidance is the single barrier to retail AI. Nor does it support the fantasy that autonomous agents are about to run merchandising, pricing, and store operations. It supports something both narrower and more actionable: access alone does not produce adoption; workflow integration matters more than generic training; trust and governance are central; and repeated use inside ordinary management routines is more likely to stick than one-off experimentation.[1][2][3][4][5]

That leaves CEOs with a less theatrical challenge than the market usually advertises. They do not need to turn every operator into an AI enthusiast. They need to decide where AI belongs in the cadence of the business, where it does not, and what standards govern its use. They need to make the first encounters safe enough to build confidence but useful enough to matter. They need to ensure that AI is owned by the business functions whose routines it is meant to change, not merely by the teams that procured the software.[2][5]

The important distinction is unlikely to be revealed by license counts, pilot volumes, or training completions alone. More serious firms will ask a duller question: in which recurring decisions is AI now expected to appear, and what happened when it did? That question lacks the romance of transformation. It has the advantage of being how operating change usually begins.
